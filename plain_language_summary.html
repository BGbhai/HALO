1. What is HALO?

HALO is a blueprint for building artificial life entirely inside a quantum computer. It combines four well-understood quantum techniques into a single architecture: a clock that runs without any external timekeeper, noise-protected agent states, memory-efficient holographic compression, and a fitness test that is computationally hard for outside observers to reverse-engineer.

The core idea is that all the machinery of evolution — time, noise protection, state compression, and fitness selection — can be implemented as sequences of quantum gates, with no classical computer stepping in between.

This document explains what has actually been demonstrated, step by step, and what remains to be done. Every claim is evaluated on a six-rung evidence ladder.

2. The Six-Rung Evidence Ladder

Rather than making a single large claim, HALO uses a claim ladder: six levels of assertions, each strictly stronger than the last. Each rung carries a declared evidence threshold — a concrete test that can falsify it. Rungs C0 through C5 have been validated; C6 is explicitly deferred to future work.

4. How Does the Quantum Evolution Work? (C5)

Imagine 8 agents placed in a quantum superposition — all of them existing in the circuit simultaneously. Each agent has an angle encoding its "fitness". Two agents (numbers 6 and 7) start near the ideal angle; the other six are farther away.

One generation of evolution applies three quantum gates in sequence:

  1. Fitness oracle — a multi-controlled phase flip that marks the two fit agents with a -1 phase, without measuring or collapsing the state.
  2. Grover diffusion — inversion-about-mean on the population register, which constructively amplifies the fit agents and destructively cancels the unfit ones.
  3. Coherent mutation — a random DFS rotation applied independently to each agent's angle, keeping the state fully coherent throughout.

The result is a characteristic oscillation: the probability of landing on a fit agent shoots up on odd generations and partially falls back on even ones — exactly the Grover over-rotation cycle. The chart below shows this pattern.

5. What Does Cross-Sector Compression Add? (C4)

The C2 result showed that quantum states in a single excitation sector (e.g., exactly 1, or exactly 2, or exactly 3 particles present at a time) can be compressed into far fewer qubits with no information loss.

C4 extends this to states that are quantum superpositions across multiple sectors at once (e.g., both 1-particle and 2-particle components simultaneously). A direct-sum isometry handles the combined subspace. For example, combining sectors {1,2,3} at N = 6 sites compresses a 41-dimensional subspace into just 6 qubits (2^6 = 64), with zero isometry error.

The leakage experiment answers a practical question: how quickly does a state leave its sector when a perturbation is switched on? The answer is clean: at zero perturbation strength, leakage is exactly zero for any evolution time. At short evolution times it grows monotonically with perturbation strength. At longer times it peaks near Γ≈0.625 then decreases — a quantum recurrence effect. Together these results give a precise quantitative boundary for when the single-sector approximation breaks down.

6. What This Paper Does Not Claim

Explicitly deferred to future work (C6 and beyond)

  • Universal holographic compression for arbitrary Hamiltonians without sector restriction
  • Cryptographic security proof against polynomial-time quantum adversaries
  • Any experiment on physical quantum hardware (all results are noiseless simulation)
  • Noise-resilient composition under realistic NISQ hardware error models
  • Multi-generation heredity, crossover, or multi-locus fitness landscapes
  • Population evolution scaling to P much larger than 8 agents

The depth tradeoff for C1 is also openly acknowledged: the binary encoding cuts qubit count by 57% on average, but increases two-qubit circuit depth by a factor of 2.9. In depth-limited hardware this tradeoff is unfavourable.

7. The Four Quantum Primitives

Every component of HALO rests on an independently-established quantum information result:

Page-Wootters clock. Instead of an external clock, a single qubit is prepared in superposition and entangled with the agent register. "Time" emerges from this entanglement: the clock value conditions whether evolution has happened or not. No classical timer is needed.

Decoherence-free subspace (DFS) encoding. Agent states live in the two-qubit subspace spanned by |01> and |10>. Collective environmental noise (same rotation on both qubits) acts as a global phase on this subspace, which is undetectable under measurement. The agent is passively immune to this noise class without active error correction.

Holographic compression. Inspired by AdS/CFT holography, an isometric map encodes a large "bulk" Hilbert space into a smaller "boundary" register. The C2 and C4 experiments make this concrete: specific subspaces are compressed into log(dim) boundary qubits with zero information loss, verified algebraically.

QMA-hard fitness. The fitness landscape is the ground-state energy of a masked local Hamiltonian. An agent with the right "genetic secret" evaluates its own fitness efficiently; an outside observer faces a problem believed to be hard for any polynomial-time quantum computer. The C3 experiments provide empirical (not complexity-theoretic) evidence for this hardening.

9. What Comes Next (C6 and Open Problems)

The six-rung ladder is a map, not just a record. The validated rungs define a solid base; the open problems define the next targets.

The most immediate next step is hardware validation: running the C0 toy circuit and at least the C5 evolution loop on a physical quantum device (trapped ion or superconducting), to see how noise affects the Grover oscillation and DFS protection in practice.

On the theory side, average-case hardness of the fitness function remains unproven. The C3 empirical trend is consistent with hardness, but a reduction from a well-studied problem (such as Learning With Errors) would be needed for a complexity-theoretic guarantee. The paper sketches this conditional reduction as a proposition in Appendix C.

Finally, multi-epoch population dynamics — heredity, crossover, multi-locus fitness — require larger circuits than the current 5-qubit toy. The C5 Grover loop is a stepping stone demonstrating that coherent selection and mutation can coexist. Scaling this to populations of 64 or more agents while preserving full coherence is a meaningful near-term target.
